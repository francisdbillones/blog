Matrices

A matrix is a 2-dimensional grid of numbers. They can be used as representations of systems of linear equations, linear mappings, and more.

They are defined as follows:

\[ M = \begin{bmatrix} a_{1, 1} & a_{1, 2} & \cdots & a_{1, c} \\ a_{2, 1} & a_{2, 2} & \cdots & a_{2, c} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ a_{r, 1} & a_{r, 2} & \cdots & a_{r, c} \end{bmatrix} \] where \(c, r \in \mathbb{N}\) and \(a\) is a \(c \cdot r\) tuple of real numbers.

A matrix can be thought of as a vector of vectors, although it may not be a mathematically sound representation:

\[ M = \begin{bmatrix} a_1 & b_1 & \cdots & z_1 \\ a_2 & b_2 & \cdots & z_2 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ a_i & b_i & \cdots & z_i \end{bmatrix} \] where \(i \in \mathbb{Z}^+\) and \(a, b, \dots,z \in R^i\)

A much more concise definition can be used using the following convention: \[ r, c \in \mathbb{N}, M \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times c} \]

Matrix addition

Matrix addition is a very simple operation, simply the element-wise sum: \[ A + B := \begin{bmatrix} A_{1, 1} + B_{1, 1} & \cdots & A_{1, c} + B_{1, c} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ A_{r, 1} + B_{r, 2} & \cdots & A_{r, c} + B_{r, c} \end{bmatrix} \] where \(r, c \in \mathbb{N}\) and \(A, B \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times c}\).

Matrix multiplication

Matrix multiplication is a less trivial operation, defined as: \[ C_{i, j} = \sum_{k=1}^{n}A_{i, k}B_{k,j} \] where \(C = AB\).

An easier way of thinking about this is that each column \(C_c\) is \(A \cdot B_c\). Then, it’s easy to think of multiplying a matrix with a column vector as the summation of scalar products with each element of the column vector and its corresponding vector in the matrix.

In effect, the matrix represents a vector space with each column vector being a basis vector in that vector space. Then, multiplying that matrix with another column vector represents the transformation of that column vector (which represents a vector in the standard vector space) considering each column of the matrix as a new basis vector.
